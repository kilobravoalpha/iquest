New CAREERs


 

   Computers are an extremely powerful tool, but they can also come with a high price tag, which presents a problem for those who would like to use a computer but can not afford the heavy price tag. The ideal solution would allow access to computers for all members of the community without any of the cost associated with computers. One way to accomplish this goal is to have communities establish public computing centers to allow access to all members of our society. This solution would permit access to and education for technology on a free basis to help reach as much of the community as possible.

   The nationwide initiative to allow public access to computers will be called CAREER, or Computer Access Resulting in Equal Employment Rights. The local plan names may vary based on location, but they should capture a local community spirit as well as a computer experience. For example, a local center in Zionsville may be named ZionNet or Zionsville Community Computer Center.

   The organizer in charge of setting up and running the center will be a local organizer. This person will be selected by the local government from among a pool of applicants. The position will be full time with a minimum starting salary of $25,000 per year. The person will be an employee of the government, and therefore will get the same benefits as other local government employees. The applicant need only have a fair amount of technical experience on computers and enough organizational skills to be capable of running such a site. The organizer's first duty will be to select a site for the computer center. Once an appropriate site has been found, this person is in charge of establishing a center and selecting a part-time staff to help. When the center nears it's opening date, the coordinator will distribute announcements in the schools and communities to publicize the availability of the computers. This person will be in charge of day-to-day operations once the center is running.

   The task of selecting a site is the first responsibility of the organizer. The most appropriate location would be in a local school, because schools often have large computer labs that have already been paid for by the government. In 1998, an estimated 89% of schools have internet access and the computers required to use it, and the number will increase to 95% by the year 2000 (Tapscott 308). If an appropriate site can not be found at a local school, the library would be an appropriate alternative. Either site already has computers paid for by the government, which means little or no equipment cost for the program.

   Each site would initially be open from 4:00 PM to 9:00 PM on Monday through Friday and from Noon until 8:00 PM on Saturdays and Sundays, though these hours may vary as needed. On the weekdays the center is open it will be staffed by the coordinator and one part-time staff member, who will earn an hourly wage of $7.00. On the weekends it will be run by two part-time employees, each making $7.00 per hour. The part-time person must have computer experience and must be able to communicate well with others. High school students looking for part-time work would be acceptable. The total for labor, including a full-time position and part-time labor, comes to $45,748 per year, or slightly more than $45,000 per year. Because expenses such as facilities operating cost and computer maintenance would be fairly small, these could be absorbed by the school or library. The total cost of the project, establishing one site for a small population of about 5,000 to 10,000 people, would be approximately $40,000. This total would be split evenly between the local and state governments, with each side paying $20,000 towards the center.

   The first step is to find funding for the project on both state and local levels. Each will be given four months to find funding, beginning in May of 1998. Funding should be available by September, at which point the local government will select a local coordinator. This should take about two months with the completion set for November. The coordinator will then select a site for the center and a part-time staff to work in the site. This process will take about two months, after which the site will open its doors. The opening date will then be January 1999.

   Each computing center will offer two introductory computing classes, one on a weekday evening, and one on the weekend. These classes will give basic orientation skills with a computer and give basic training in how to use an operating system and a word processor. These classes will be two hours in length and will last for one month, and will be taught by one of the two employees who are always on duty. A second class of the same length and times will offer orientation toward the internet and e-mail. Orientation for other software will occur in one hour sessions throughout the week.

   Currently, the heavy cost associated with computers does not allow access to disadvantaged people who cannot afford one. The computing center plan solves this problem by allowing access to computers while foregoing the high cost, effectively presenting an equalizer to help the disadvantaged to keep up with those who own computers.

   The setup for this plan is fairly simple. Once proper funding is obtained, the only remaining obstacle is to find an appropriate site. Using a public school computer lab is not unreasonable and provides a nearly ideal alternative to owning a computer. Because the program encourages computer literacy at no cost, it would be able to draw in people. Encouraging computer literacy also promotes worker productivity, which would help the program to pay for itself in the long run. Because the project requires a relatively small amount of funding, this step should not present a challenge.

   This plan allows anyone who wants access to a computer to have a computer and a tutor at their disposal at no cost. Providing computer skills to the community gives them an advantage in the job market, which often requires at least some knowledge of computers for almost any job. Providing access to internet and e-mail allows people to communicate around the world as well as performing research. This plan also provides a simple and cost-effective solution, which is desirable in every case.

   The simplicity of this program makes it practical. The idea of providing a relatively small number of computers to a group makes much more sense than providing each person, which has been proposed in the past. Fewer computers means less expense, less maintenance, and a better use of resources. Putting a computing center in a public place would make access convenient to everyone. This solution provides the most practical alternative to solving the problem of unequal access to computers.

   Some people may argue that people would not come to such a computer center. This can be proven false, however, by looking at examples in other areas. A similar plan was executed in East Palo Alto, in which a community center was established to give the community free access to computers and to the internet. Dominic Bannister, a 15 year old boy who often visits the center, described the advantages. "I think they should have a place like Plugged-In in every community, especially in troubled neighborhoods. Here, this place has helped the community a lot and it's a great place to be at" (Tapscott 269). The center was a huge success and it was even able to make a lasting positive impression on the community.

   Solving the problem of how to increase adult computer literacy would yield enormous benefits. Establishing a site such as described above allows for and encourages computer literacy in the adult population of a community. These skills increase productivity and may allow for new employment opportunity. The cost for the community relative to the benefits and opportunities is very small, making this plan the most economical and practical solution of all.

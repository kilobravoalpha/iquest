    Whatever to all mankind is assured,
    I, in my inmost being, will enjoy and know,
    Seize with my soul the highest and most deep;
    Men's weal and woe upon my bosom heap;
    And thus this self of mine to all their selves expanded,
    Like them I too at last be stranded.

        -Mephistopheles


 

   Every year at this time the snows began to fall. It was something that they had grown to live with in Apple Valley, Minnesota. As Mark Wilberg sat in his bedroom and looked contemplatively out the window, he couldn't help but wonder if this year would be like all of the others.

   Mark had been born and raised in Apple Valley, a fairly affluent suburb of Minneapolis. While he never considered himself to be rich, Mark's parents were usually able to get him whatever he wanted. He attended Adams High School and couldn't wait to go off to college next year. When he wasn't at home or school, he could almost always be found at his best friend Josh's house. The two shared an intense interest in information appliances, what we would have called computers.

   One day the two found themselves in the middle of a particularly boring lecture in their Modern History class. The teacher was talking about the informational revolution of the first decade of the still new millennia. The idea seemed about as removed to them as the industrial revolution, and the whole class seemed to be somewhere between absent-minded daydreaming and REM sleep. After some twenty years of teaching their instructor expected this reaction, and so he decided to teach his class a lesson.

   "Imagine yourself without all of the technology that you take for granted," Mr. Grant began. "Without your netphones and pocketnets, your high definition televisions, your digital walkmans, life would not be the same."

   "We've lived thousands and even millions of years without this stuff," Mark interjected. "I don't see what the big deal is. Just because we have this technology, it doesn't change us as people."

   "I think I'll let you find that out for yourself," Mr. Grant said. "Here is your assignment. For the next week you can't use any of our new digital machines. That includes information appliances, televisions, the Internet, and almost anything with an on switch."

   Mike and Josh were discussing the assignment at lunch that day.

   "I still don't think it's a big deal," Mark started. "I mean, It's fun to watch TV and use the 'net and all, but how hard can it be to go without it?"

   "I don't know, Mark," Josh replied. "I'm used to being able to use it. I think it could be pretty hard to go without it."

   Mark made his first discovery when he went home that afternoon. He sat down, following his daily routine, with a bag of microwaved popcorn to watch TV. As he reached for the remote, he remembered the assignment. 'No problem,' he thought. 'I'll just give Josh a call, he'll find something to do.' But the phone was off limits, too. Mark hopped onto his long-forgotten bicycle and pedaled his way over to Josh's house.

   Normally the two would play a virtual game on Josh's system, but now that activity was off-limits too. The two finally settled on an old board game they found in the basement, though this seemed quite boring in comparison with their usual activity on the virtual game system.

   Mark ran into many other problems later that week. He had to drag an old typewriter out of the attic to finish an English report, which he received a poor grade on due to the lack of a built-in spelling and grammar checker. He had to research Saturn for his science class, but because his webphone was off-limits he had to resort to using an old book at the library. Worst of all, Mark had to clean up after himself because the automaid was of no use.

   One week after the assignment had been given. Mark wanted badly to use a digital machine again. Even if it was just the webphone, he couldn't wait until he would be allowed access again. Once class started, Mr. Grant asked his students how they had fared.

   "I guess you were right, Mr. Grant," Mark admitted. "It was very hard to do without the technology of the Information Age. But I still don't see how any of this is relevant. I mean, now that we do have the technology, why does it matter how different it might be to live without it?"

   "That's exactly my point, Mark," Mr. Grant said excitedly. "Life was very different without all of the technology that you take for granted. Now, imagine that you had never even been exposed to the technology in the first place. No webphones, no virtual video games, not even a word processor. Did any of you find it harder to do your school work this week?"

   "Yea," Mark responded for the class. "I guess it was a little harder without my word processor for English and the Internet for Science. But what's your point?"

   "I'm getting there, just wait," Mr. Grant answered. "You found it harder to do the work without the aid of technology. What about those who don't have access to the technology? Do you think that puts them at a disadvantage?"

   "I think I see your point now," Mark replied. "There are people who don't have information appliances, and I suppose that puts them at a disadvantage. But the problem is so big! What could we possibly do to fix the problem?"

   After a moment of thought, Mr. Grant answered, "that's up to you."
